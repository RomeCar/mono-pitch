§ 01 — the european partnerships problem

Pipeline doesn't compound. Trust does.

Most partnerships orgs in Europe measure themselves on outbound volume — meetings booked, decks sent, MQLs surfaced. The numbers move; the deals don't. The reason is structural: Europe is twenty-seven jurisdictions, four big buyer cultures, and a procurement process that rewards relationships built over years rather than quarters.

The teams that close at scale here look different. They have a small senior bench with deep operator credibility in two or three target verticals, they reinvest in those verticals rather than chase pipeline breadth, and they treat reference accounts as a renewable resource — every closed customer becomes the introduction path for the next three. The compounding is in the network, not the funnel.

The interesting question for Northwind in the next eighteen months isn't "how do we generate more pipeline" — it's "which two verticals do we go deep on, and which buyers do we earn the right to be the default partner for." That's a different operating model and a different hire.

three concrete moves for the first six months
  1. 01 ▸ Pick two verticals, exit the rest. Most European partnerships orgs run nine and close one. Northwind's product is sharpest in regulated industries with long procurement cycles — start with financial services and energy, defer the others.
  2. 02 ▸ Build a senior reference bench. Five named buyers per vertical, willing to take a 30-minute reference call. Make this a measured KPI, not a lagging metric — it predicts close rate twelve months out.
  3. 03 ▸ Co-develop with two systems integrators. European mid-market doesn't buy direct from American tech. Pick two regional SIs (one Nordic, one DACH), build a real co-sell motion, treat them as channel rather than as resellers.

None of this is novel. Stripe, Snowflake and Databricks all ran a version of it in their European push. The differentiator is execution speed.
